# Social Engineering Attacks
## Report for Oasis Infobyte Security Analyst Internship (Task 5)

### 1. Introduction

Social engineering is the psychological manipulation of people into performing actions or divulging confidential information. Unlike technical hacking, social engineering exploits human psychology rather than system vulnerabilities.

### 2. Phishing

**What is Phishing?**
Phishing is a type of social engineering attack where attackers send fraudulent communications that appear to come from a legitimate source.

**Common Types:**
- **Spear Phishing:** Targeted attacks on specific individuals (e.g., a CEO).
- **Whaling:** Attacks targeting senior executives (e.g., a "CEO fraud" email).
- **Vishing:** Phishing via phone calls (voice).
- **Smishing:** Phishing via SMS text messages.

**Real-World Case Study (2011 RSA Attack):**
Attackers sent a spear-phishing email with a malicious Excel attachment to RSA employees. Two employees opened it, leading to the compromise of RSA's SecurID two-factor authentication tokens.

**Prevention Recommendations:**
1.  Conduct regular security awareness training for all employees.
2.  Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) to protect against stolen credentials.
3.  Use email filtering and anti-phishing tools.
4.  Verify any sensitive requests via a different communication channel.

### 3. Pretexting

**What is Pretexting?**
Pretexting involves creating a fabricated scenario (the "pretext") to manipulate a victim into providing information, often by impersonating a trusted figure like IT support or a bank official.

**Prevention Measures:**
1.  Always verify the identity of the person requesting information.
2.  Avoid disclosing sensitive information over the phone unless you initiated the call.
3.  Use callbacks – hang up and call the organization back on a known, verified number.

### 4. Baiting

**What is Baiting?**
Baiting uses the promise of a good or service (like a free download or an infected USB drive) to trick the victim into performing a specific action.

**Prevention Measures:**
1.  Implement a strict security policy banning the use of untrusted USB drives.
2.  Educate employees about the risks of using unknown physical or digital devices.
3.  Configure systems to disable auto-run for removable media.

### 5. Quid Pro Quo

**What is Quid Pro Quo?**
Meaning "something for something," this attack involves the attacker offering a service (e.g., "I can fix your computer issue") in exchange for sensitive information, such as login credentials.

**Prevention:**
1.  Be suspicious of unsolicited offers or requests for information.
2.  Always verify the identity and credentials of any outside party offering assistance.
3.  Report suspicious attempts to the security team immediately.

### 6. Organizational Recommendations

**Employee Security Awareness Training Checklist:**
1.  **Identify Phishing:** Spot suspicious emails and links.
2.  **Report Immediately:** Establish a clear process for reporting attacks.
3.  **Verify Requests:** Confirm sensitive requests through a known channel.
4.  **Use Trusted Channels:** Never share sensitive info unless you initiated the contact.
5.  **Think Before Clicking:** Always think before clicking links or opening attachments.

### 7. Conclusion

Social engineering attacks are highly effective because they target human psychology rather than technology. The best defense is a combination of robust security policies, regular employee training, and a vigilant security culture.
